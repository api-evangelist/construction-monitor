<p>In Egypt and Babylon, craftsmanship training was organized to maintain an adequate number of skilled trades. The 18th century&nbsp;BCE&nbsp;<a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Code-of-Hammurabi">Code of Hammurabi of Babylon</a>&nbsp;required craftsmen to teach their skills to the next generation. And U.S. construction industry skilled trades have a long tradition; George Washington was an apprentice land surveyor.</p>



<p class="has-text-align-center"><em>As the workforce continues to reinvent itself, there has never been a better moment to start bridging the awareness gap between Gen-Z and a rewarding career in the skilled trades.</em> &#8211;<a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/markcperna/2022/04/05/4-research-based-strategies-to-make-skilled-trade-careers-more-attractive-to-gen-z/?sh=1a063c0f68ff">Mark C. Perna</a></p>



<p>Even though skilled trades are changing rapidly with technological advances, young people continue to have obsolete ideas about skilled trades careers. A <a href="https://www.stanleyblackanddecker.com/impact/people/makers-index">StanleyBlack&amp;Decker survey</a> revealed one-fourth of high school students believe skilled trades don’t use cutting-edge technology. The knowledge gaps revealed by the survey suggest many young people, their parents, and school career counselors don’t know the benefits of skilled trade careers. For example, the <a href="https://www.tradesnation.com/us/en/the-trades-facts-and-figures.html">total cost of a trade school education</a> averages the cost of one year’s tuition in a 4-year undergraduate program.</p>



<p>Here are 4 strategies to promote skilled trades careers in construction by overcoming perceptions:</p>



<p>1. Accessibility perception:&nbsp;Skilled trades involve difficult, manual labor.</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Reality:&nbsp;Most students are concerned about their inability to master the necessary skills.</li>



<li>Strategy:&nbsp;Create opportunities for students to discover that physical work can be rewarding. Allow them to experience “getting their hands dirty” before deciding against a skilled trade&nbsp;career path.</li>
</ul>



<p>2. Exposure perception:&nbsp;School career counseling will give students what they need to know about skilled trades.</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Reality:&nbsp;About 37% of students have never spoken with a counselor or skilled tradesperson at all.</li>



<li>Strategy: Employers can use print, social, and other media to influence career-planning Gen-Z students.</li>
</ul>



<p>3. Financial stability perception:&nbsp;Skilled trades don’t pay enough.</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Reality:&nbsp;After 5 years, a trades school graduate is $140,000 ahead of a 4-year college student.</li>



<li>Strategy:&nbsp;Educate more students about the financial advantages of skilled trades pathways.</li>
</ul>



<p>4. Gender equality perception: Skilled trades are better for men than women.</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Reality:&nbsp;More than ever, women are finding fulfilling careers in skilled trades.</li>



<li>Strategy:&nbsp;Employers can increase awareness among students and their parents that young women are finding skilled trades a rewarding career choice using school college fairs, etc.</li>
</ul>
